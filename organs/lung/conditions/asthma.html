Lung-Respiratory System · Conditions

Asthma

Reactive airway disease
Overview

Variable, reversible airflow obstruction. Presents differently depending on trigger, chronicity, and predominant physiology — which is exactly why it maps to several different patterns rather than one.

Predominant Patterns
  • Qi Excess — Constriction (Qi Stagnation) — Primary: cough-variant and stress/emotion-triggered bronchospasm map most directly onto Qi Excess — TRPV1/TRPA1-driven hyperreactivity without a strong inflammatory or structural component.
  • Yang Deficiency — Internal · Lower Respiratory · Cold — Common in chronic disease: persistent, poorly-responsive "wet" asthma with a thyroid/kidney-yang component — named directly in that pattern's Biomedical Correlates.
  • Yang Deficiency — External · Upper Respiratory · Wind-Cold — Trigger-phase: cold-air-induced flares are named directly in that pattern's correlates — useful when a patient's asthma reliably worsens with cold exposure.
Anthroposophic Therapeutics

Bronchial asthma is read as a cramping ("astral") phenomenon: the astral body, which must normally sway back and forth through the rhythmic system without becoming too strong, instead penetrates the rhythmic system with the dynamics of the nerve-sense pole — working one-sidedly into lung tissue without the mediating intervention of the ether body. These cramping episodes are often secondary, an attempt by the body to secrete or eliminate substances. Triggers (feathers, hair, pollens) bear a direct relation to the astral body — foreign substances penetrate the organism and the body reacts allergically in self-protection; emotional triggers and shock (including shock experienced by the mother in utero) can likewise cramp the astral body. Kidney function is considered primary here — asthma is treated in this framework as a functional disease of the kidney system, with the lung as the secondary organ; the astral is properly inserted into metabolism via the kidney.

Glucocorticoids / adrenaline — can temporarily replace deficient kidney activity
Steiner-indicated: Prunus spinosa D3 or D5 (injection, neck region); Nicotiana tabacum D6 or D10 (injection, kidney area); Gencydo 1%, 3% (between shoulder blades) — daily or weekly injections
Other remedies: Quercus 10% (AM); Veronica 10% (PM); Argentum nitricum D20 (dry asthma); Cuprum aceticum D3, D4 or Cuprum/ren (copper); carbonic acid baths; acid therapy (Gencydo, Prunus, formic acid, carbonic acid)
In children: Phosphorus D5 or D6 (replaces Quercus, AM); Cuprum per melissam or Quartz D12-D20 (replaces Veronica, PM); Belladonna (related to copper); Formica D6, 10, 15, 30 or sulfur baths (by injection, to counter strong hardening processes)
