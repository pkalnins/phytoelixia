Lung-Respiratory System

Conditions

Start from a diagnosis you already recognize, then follow a link into its predominant pattern(s).

As explored in the Foundations chapter, Yang, Qi, Blood, and Yin can be read not just as four parallel categories but as stations along a single depth progression — from superficial and reversible toward deep and structural — echoing both Steiner's polarity between metabolic (inflammatory) and sclerotic (degenerative) illness, and Reckeweg's six-phase homotoxicology table, where a "biological division" separates the humoral phases (still extracellular, still reversible) from the cellular phases (intracellular, progressively less reversible).

Read this way, the conditions below aren't an arbitrary list — they trace one plausible path along that same progression, specifically as it plays out in the lung. And it turns out this isn't just an analogy this workbook is imposing from outside: Reckeweg's own teaching materials include a "Respiratory Tract" row in his six-phase table, naming a specific disease example at every phase —

Excretion Reaction/Inflammation Deposition Impregnation Degeneration Dedifferentiation
Cough, expectoration Bronchitis, acute Silicosis, smoker's lung Chronic (obstructive) bronchitis Bronchiectasis, emphysema Bronchial carcinoma

(Source: International Academy of Homotoxicology, Introduction to Homotoxicology, IAH 2007.) That row lines up with the progression below closely enough to treat as real corroboration, not coincidence — with two refinements worth naming explicitly.

  • Common Cold and the acute phase of Pneumonia sit in the two most superficial Yang-metabolic phases — Excretion and Reaction/Inflammation — matching Reckeweg's own "Cough, expectoration" and "Bronchitis, acute" almost verbatim. The organism is actively, forcefully clearing an acute insult; nothing here is fixed yet.
  • Asthma is better read as straddling Deposition and Impregnation rather than sitting cleanly in one or the other — and Reckeweg's own table supports that straddle rather than contradicting it. "Silicosis, smoker's lung" (Deposition) is a chronic irritant-driven change that's still substantially matrix/humoral; "Chronic (obstructive) bronchitis" (Impregnation) is the first clearly cellular entry, and it's also where Reckeweg's prose separately places asthma and hay fever together as classic Impregnation-phase diseases (long quiet latency, punctuated by a disproportionate reaction to a minor trigger). Clinically this fits too: bronchospasm alone is functional and reversible (Deposition-like), while the airway remodeling and smooth-muscle hypertrophy seen in chronic, severe asthma is structural and cellular (Impregnation-like) — asthma has both an ECM component and a genuinely cellular one, which is exactly why a single-phase placement undersold it.
  • COPD turns out to span three phases, not one, in Reckeweg's own table — and this is the richest single point of agreement with the model below. Its three named entries trace almost exactly the clinical course of the disease: "Silicosis, smoker's lung" (Deposition) as the early, still-humoral irritant load; "Chronic (obstructive) bronchitis" (Impregnation) as the disease crosses into the cell; "Bronchiectasis, emphysema" (Degeneration) as structural, largely irreversible tissue damage. So COPD isn't simply "Blood" — it's the segment of the progression that runs Qi → Blood → Yin, depending on stage and phenotype.
  • Pulmonary Fibrosis sits at the deepest, most "Yin" end of this progression. Reckeweg's table doesn't name pulmonary fibrosis specifically, but "Bronchiectasis, emphysema" at Degeneration is the same territory — progressive, largely irreversible structural damage — so the placement holds by strong analogy even without an exact-name match.
  • Lung Cancer, not yet its own entry in this reference, is this progression's furthest point — and here Reckeweg's table gives an exact match: "Bronchial carcinoma" at Dedifferentiation, where cells lose their differentiated identity altogether. Worth adding as a full condition once that pattern material exists.

Where Allergic Rhinitis fits, per Reckeweg himself, is also answerable rather than open: the IAH's own teaching materials name hay fever explicitly as a classic example of the Impregnation phase, grouped there with asthma, migraine, and gastric ulcer — the same "long quiet latency, disproportionate reaction" pattern described above. Reckeweg's six-phase table also lists "Allergy" generally at Impregnation, under the Skin organ-system row. With Asthma now placed as straddling Deposition/Impregnation rather than sitting at Deposition alone, this is no longer a tension to flag — Allergic Rhinitis and the cellular half of Asthma's range now sit at the same phase, which is consistent with how closely the two conditions travel together clinically.

Overall, the Yang → Qi → Blood → Yin reading holds up well against Reckeweg's own classifications — better, in fact, than a single-phase-per-condition model could show, once COPD and Asthma are read as spanning phases rather than occupying just one.

One distinction is still worth naming, though: Reckeweg is classifying the disease label homotoxicologically, while this workbook's Predominant Patterns (below, per condition) classify the clinical presentation by TCM pattern — a single disease can present as more than one pattern, which is exactly why, for example, Asthma below maps to three different patterns rather than one. The two schemes describe different things and are meant to be read alongside each other, not collapsed into one.
