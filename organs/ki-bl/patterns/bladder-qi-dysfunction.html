Kidney-Bladder System · Patterns

Bladder Qi Dysfunction

Qi Excess
Primary Biological Axis

Autonomic (Qi Axis): Detrusor and pelvic autonomic hyperreactivity without an infectious driver.

Pathophysiology

Detrusor muscle overactivity, autonomic/parasympathetic hyperreactivity of the bladder wall, and heightened visceral afferent sensitivity — functionally analogous to Liver Qi Stagnation's visceral hypersensitivity mechanism, but localized to the bladder.

Gene / Signaling Pathways

Elevated M2/M3 muscarinic receptor sensitivity in detrusor smooth muscle, elevated TRPV1 expression in bladder afferents, reduced central GABAergic inhibition of the micturition reflex.

Key Symptoms & Clinical Findings

Urinary urgency and frequency without infection, spasmodic bladder discomfort, a sensation of incomplete emptying, and symptoms that fluctuate directly with stress or anxiety.

Tongue

Normal body, thin white coating.

Pulse

Wiry (Xian), occasionally normal between flares.

Temporal / Qualitative Patterns

Episodic, fluctuates with stress and anxiety. Worse with caffeine, alcohol, or cold exposure.
> stress reduction, warmth, bladder training
< stress, caffeine, alcohol, cold exposure

Musculoskeletal / Pain Expression

Suprapubic tension or discomfort, pelvic floor tightness, occasional referred low back tension.

Psychological / Consciousness Expression

Anxiety concentrated around urinary urgency and access to a bathroom, hypervigilance toward bladder sensation, a self-reinforcing loop between anxiety and symptom flares.

Biomedical Correlates

Overactive bladder (OAB), interstitial cystitis/bladder pain syndrome (non-infectious), functional or neurogenic bladder dysfunction, stress urinary incontinence with a detrusor-overactivity component.

Confirming Lab Markers

Urinalysis: Typically clean/negative for infection, distinguishing this from Bladder Damp-Heat.

Urodynamic Testing: May show detrusor overactivity on cystometry where formally evaluated.
