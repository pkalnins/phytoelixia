The Reckeweg Model

The twentieth-century physician Hans-Heinrich Reckeweg, working independently in his system of homotoxicology, described a granular six-phase version of the metabolic/sclerotic movement introduced in Module 9 — the body's escalating response to a "homotoxin" (any burden, internal or external, the organism must process):

Phase Layer Character
1. Excretion Humoral Toxin cleared through normal channels — sweat, urine, mucus. Superficial, self-limited.
2. Reaction (Inflammation) Humoral Active immune/inflammatory defense — fever, acute inflammation.
3. Deposition Humoral Un-excreted toxin deposited in connective tissue — still extracellular, still substantially reversible.
— the Biological Division — The critical threshold. Below it, illness is still fundamentally a defensive, self-limiting process. Above it, the disease process enters the cell itself.
4. Impregnation Cellular Toxin penetrates the cell, disturbing enzyme systems and function without yet destroying structure.
5. Degeneration Cellular Structural damage to the cell's organelles — mitochondria especially — and to tissue architecture generally; the cell's own energy-production machinery begins to fail.
6. Dedifferentiation Cellular Cells lose their differentiated identity — the most severe, least reversible phase. Malignancy is the classic example.

Modern biology gives that fifth phase a specific address. Reckeweg named it decades before the research existed, but "Degeneration" lines up remarkably well with a genuinely modern finding: across essentially every degenerative disease studied — neurodegeneration, COPD, pulmonary fibrosis, cardiac failure, aging itself — impaired mitochondrial function turns up as a common, often causal, thread. The organelle Reckeweg's phase points to structurally is the same one the current research keeps landing on mechanistically.

Reckeweg's own clinical concern was that suppressing a humoral-phase illness — treating it in a way that blocks the excretory or reactive process rather than letting it complete — can drive the disease process deeper, pushing it across the biological division into the cellular phases. He called this vicariation.

This workbook's own synthesis here (not a claim about what either Steiner or Reckeweg themselves proposed) is that Yang, Qi, Blood, and Yin — this workbook's four-ether correlates, established in Module 6, and the general depth progression introduced in Module 9 — can be given Reckeweg's own specific six-phase form:

Category Reckeweg Phase(s) Character
Yang Excretion, Reaction/Inflammation Superficial, humoral, actively metabolic — the two most acute phases.
Qi Deposition Still humoral, but semi-fixed or recurring rather than a single self-limited event — the threshold phase, right at the biological division.
— the Biological Division —
Blood Impregnation, early Degeneration Crossing into the cell — first the nutritive/cytoplasmic layer, then the organelles, with mitochondrial function often the first casualty.
Yin Degeneration, Dedifferentiation The deepest layer — mitochondrial and structural collapse at one extreme, loss of differentiated cell identity at the other.

Read this way, a pattern isn't only a snapshot of an organ's current state — it can also mark a position along a trajectory, and suppressive rather than resolving treatment at any stage risks driving that trajectory deeper. Each organ's own Conditions section, where one exists, works through this progression in concrete, organ-specific detail — see the Lung's for a worked example.

The Qi/Deposition pairing above is testable, not just an analogy, because this workbook already identifies Qi with autonomic/neural coordination (the Light Ether, established in Module 6) and Deposition with Pischinger's ground regulation system — and that system is itself explicitly defined as three structural components: the terminal vascular pathway, the connective tissue, and the terminal vegetative-nervous (autonomic) pathway.2 Autonomic dysregulation isn't just structurally implied here, either: reduced heart-rate variability — vagal withdrawal alongside relative sympathetic predominance — tracks directly with the severity of atherosclerosis (Reckeweg's own Deposition-phase cardiovascular example) even before symptoms appear,7 is one of the best-established features of metabolic syndrome (his Deposition-phase example under general metabolism),8 and shows up early in smokers, deepening progressively as smoking-related lung disease advances.9 There's also a plausible mechanism for why Deposition's burden stays walled off rather than resolving: lymphatic vessels are directly sympathetically innervated, and it's specifically chronic (not acute) sympathetic tone that the literature ties to impaired drainage — via stress-associated fibroblast-to-myofibroblast tissue remodeling that physically entraps material, the same process this workbook already names as Deposition's core mechanism.10 The Lung module's own Physiology section works through both threads in organ-specific detail, including an important caveat about this second mechanism not being a universal rule.

The Body's Own Defense, Phase by Phase

"Support, don't suppress" (Module 9) stays a slogan unless it names something concrete to support. Each of Reckeweg's six phases turns out to correspond to a specific, well-characterized physiological process the organism uses to defend itself — mapping them makes the salutogenic principle usable at the bedside, phase by phase:

Phase The Body's Own Defense Immune System Correlate4
1. Excretion Elimination via the emunctories — sweat, mucus, renal and hepatic clearance, including Phase I/II hepatic biotransformation that prepares compounds for excretion. Innate, barrier immunity — mucosal and skin defenses (secretory IgA, antimicrobial peptides, mucociliary clearance) doing the first-line work of keeping the homotoxin from penetrating further.
2. Reaction (Inflammation) Acute innate immunity — fever, phagocytosis (neutrophils, macrophages), complement activation, cytokine signaling. The innate response opens this phase, but the clearance work is substantially adaptive: antigen presentation drives T- and B-cell clonal expansion over the following days,5 with cytotoxic T cells and antibodies doing the specific work of eliminating the pathogen or toxin. A successful resolution is marked by regulatory T cells (Tregs) expanding to close the response down and restore tolerance;6 it's the completion of this adaptive arc, not the initial innate trigger, that determines whether Phase 2 fully resolves. Failure to complete it — not any deficiency — is what carries the process forward into Deposition.
3. Deposition Sequestration in the extracellular matrix — Pischinger's "ground regulation system": fibroblasts and connective tissue buffer and wall off the toxin outside the cell, with lymphatic drainage as the intended exit route. A shift from active, antigen-specific clearance to chronic, low-grade inflammatory containment — the burden that Reaction's T- and B-cell response couldn't fully clear (an incomplete resolution, often reflecting insufficient Treg-mediated contraction) now gets walled off rather than removed, consistent with Deposition's other organ-row examples (coronary heart disease, cerebrosclerosis).
4. Impregnation Intracellular clearance — autophagy and lysosomal degradation (including chaperone-mediated autophagy), and the Nrf2–Keap1–ARE antioxidant/xenobiotic-response pathway, working to process what has now entered the cell. Adaptive immune dysregulation proper — this is where autoimmune disease, immunodeficiency, and chronic infection appear as Reckeweg's own examples for this phase. The adaptive machinery active since Phase 2 now turns disordered or chronically engaged rather than resolving — and this is where autoimmunity itself sits, not spread across the deeper phases.
5. Degeneration Mitochondrial quality control — mitophagy (PINK1/Parkin-mediated clearance of damaged mitochondria) and mitochondrial biogenesis, attempting to replace what structural damage has destroyed. Structural damage to the immune system's own cells, rather than continued dysregulated attack — Reckeweg's example here is AIDS: the same organelle/structural damage described for this phase generally, now happening to the immune system's own cellular machinery.
6. Dedifferentiation Immune surveillance — NK cells and cytotoxic T-cells identifying and clearing aberrant cells, backed by p53-mediated apoptosis and tumor-suppressor activity. Loss of a coordinated, specific response altogether — most plausibly reflecting delayed-type (cell-mediated) hypersensitivity as the last, least-specific residue of cellular immune activity. Least certain of the six readings; the source is a translated teaching text and shouldn't be taken as settled.

(The individual mechanisms are well-established, independently of homotoxicology: autophagy's role in clearing xenobiotic burden is established toxicology,1 the extracellular matrix's role as a toxin-buffering "ground system" is Pischinger's own foundational work as developed by Reckeweg,2 and mitophagy is the primary recognized mechanism of mitochondrial quality control.3 The phase-by-phase pairing in the middle column is this workbook's own synthesis, not a claim drawn directly from Reckeweg's writing. The Immune System Correlate column draws its phase-to-disease anchors (autoimmune disease, AIDS) from Reckeweg's own "Immune System" organ-row in the same six-phase table,4 but is written here in this workbook's own descriptive terms throughout, in keeping with this table's actual point: Reaction is the body's correct, usually successful attempt to clear the homotoxin outright, and it is specifically an incomplete clearance at that phase — not any deficiency in the immune system itself — that lets the process advance to Deposition. This is the same vicariation logic already described above, applied here specifically to the immune system. Reaction's specific description as an innate-then-adaptive arc reflects standard immunology rather than Reckeweg's own writing: primary adaptive responses take roughly five to seven days to develop,5 and regulatory T cells are well established as the mechanism that resolves acute inflammation and restores tolerance once a response succeeds.6)

Read this way, a treatment's job at any given phase is to keep the relevant mechanism working, not to shut it down: supporting excretion rather than suppressing it, letting a fever complete its course rather than reflexively lowering it, supporting lymphatic drainage at Deposition, supporting autophagy and antioxidant capacity at Impregnation, mitochondrial function at Degeneration, immune surveillance at Dedifferentiation. This is also, very plausibly, what much of this workbook's own herbal reference is already doing without having said so explicitly: diaphoretics and lymphatics for Yang/Qi-territory patterns, hepatoprotective and antioxidant herbs for Blood-territory patterns, mitochondrial-supportive nutrients for the deepest Yin patterns read less like symptom management and more like targeted support for whichever of these six defense mechanisms is currently doing the work. That's a hypothesis, not yet a verified claim about every herb in this reference — but it is a testable one, worth checking pattern by pattern as the Physiology and Pattern Reference sections are refined further.

Supporting Each Stage: Matching Patterns to Phases

"Support, don't suppress" becomes directly actionable once a presentation is matched to both a Reckeweg phase and the clinical pattern(s) that phase corresponds to within a given organ's own Pattern Reference. Each organ's Patterns section already documents, pattern by pattern, the symptoms, tongue, pulse, lab markers, and multi-tradition treatment directions for exactly this purpose — so once the phase is identified, the treatment doesn't need to be invented: it's simply the treatment already written down for the matching pattern.

Two nuances are worth naming before this mapping gets treated as fixed or universal. First, a phase doesn't always correspond to exactly one pattern — Deposition and Degeneration in particular tend to split into two patterns apiece, representing two different qualitative directions the same phase can take: matter/substance accumulation versus functional/nervous-system dysregulation at Deposition, and metabolic collapse versus structural collapse at Degeneration (echoing Steiner's own metabolic/sclerotic polarity, described in Module 9, now playing out within a single Reckeweg phase rather than only across the whole six-phase span). Second, not every phase will have a matching pattern yet in every organ's own reference. Dedifferentiation — the deepest, least reversible phase — is the one most likely to be missing a pattern, since it corresponds to malignancy, and this workbook's Pattern Reference is built primarily around functional, herbally-treatable presentations rather than oncological ones. Where that gap exists, it should be left open rather than forced closed.

Worked Example: The Lung

Yang Excess itself divides into two subtypes for the Lung — superficial (Wind: Wind-Cold, Wind-Heat) and systemic (Heat) — and the clearest reading of Phase 1 and Phase 2 together is a three-step escalation through those subtypes: Wind-Cold, then Wind-Heat, then Heat, tracking the progression from a superficial cold-natured pathogen through a superficial heat-transformed one to a fully systemic, established infection. This isn't always a clean linear path, though — pathogens can also move directly from a cold-natured superficial presentation into the lower respiratory tract without transforming into a heat pattern first (a "cold" acute bronchitis, still cold-natured but now systemic), so the escalation should be read as the typical route rather than the only one.

Reckeweg Phase Lung Pattern(s) Note
1. Excretion Yang Excess · Upper Respiratory · Wind-Cold, escalating toward Yang Excess · Upper Respiratory · Wind-Heat Wind-Cold matches that pattern's own Biomedical Correlates almost exactly ("early incubation phase of common cold") — the superficial, cold-natured opening stage. Wind-Heat is the superficial heat-transformed stage that typically follows, sitting right at this phase's boundary with Reaction/Inflammation.
2. Reaction/Inflammation Yang Excess · Lower Respiratory · Heat The systemic subtype — matches Reckeweg's own "Bronchitis, acute" example and beyond; this pattern's own correlates name pneumonia and acute lung abscess directly.
3. Deposition Qi Excess/Constriction + Yin Excess/Phlegm-Damp Splits by direction: the functional/autonomic side (Qi Excess — this pattern's own Gene/Signaling field already names reduced HRV and elevated catecholamines) and the substance/matter side (Phlegm-Damp) — together instantiating the two non-vascular components of Pischinger's own ground regulation system.
4. Impregnation Qi Deficiency Correlates name COPD and allergic rhinitis directly, matching Reckeweg's own "Chronic (obstructive) bronchitis" placement and the Allergic Rhinitis placement established in the Lung's Conditions section.
5. Degeneration Yang Deficiency · Lower Respiratory · Cold + Yin Deficiency · Internal · Chronic Dryness Splits by Steiner's metabolic/sclerotic polarity: mitochondrial/metabolic collapse (this pattern's own correlates name "mitochondrial decay" and senile emphysema) versus structural/fibrotic collapse (correlates name Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis and chronic cellular senescence) — both landing at the same phase from opposite directions.
6. Dedifferentiation (open) No Lung pattern yet corresponds to this phase — consistent with Lung Cancer not yet being its own entry in the Conditions section.

How to Read the Organ Chapters

Each organ chapter that follows this one opens with:

  1. Phenomenology — observing the organ's etheric gesture (and, where relevant, its astral/consciousness dimension) through direct, attentive description rather than mechanism.
  2. Physiology — translating that etheric activity into biomedical and systems-biology terms, organized by the organ's own Yang, Qi, Blood, and Yin, plus its axes of relationship to other organs.
  3. Pattern Reference — the clinical instantiation of all of the above: symptoms, tongue, pulse, lab markers, and treatment directions across multiple healing traditions, organ-pattern by organ-pattern.

The three are one movement, read at increasing resolution — from gesture, to mechanism, to clinical application.

The Reckeweg Model: Six Phases of Homotoxicosis

  • Reckeweg's homotoxicology, six phases (body's escalating response to a "homotoxin"), giving Reckeweg's own specific form to the general depth progression introduced in Module 9:
    • Humoral (reversible): 1) Excretion — normal-channel clearance (sweat/urine/mucus). 2) Reaction/Inflammation — active immune defense, fever. 3) Deposition — un-excreted toxin stored in connective tissue.
    • The Biological Division — the critical threshold between defensive/reversible and cellular/less-reversible illness.
    • Cellular (progressively less reversible): 4) Impregnation — toxin penetrates the cell, function disturbed. 5) Degeneration — structural damage to cells/organelles, mitochondria especially — cellular energy production begins to fail. 6) Dedifferentiation — cells lose differentiated identity (malignancy).
  • Modern echo: across essentially every degenerative disease studied (neurodegeneration, COPD, pulmonary fibrosis, cardiac failure, aging) — impaired mitochondrial function is a common, often causal, thread. Reckeweg's Degeneration phase, named decades earlier, lines up with this mechanistically.

    • Yang ↔ Excretion + Reaction/Inflammation — superficial, humoral, acute.
    • Qi ↔ Deposition — humoral but semi-fixed/recurring; the threshold phase.

    • Blood ↔ Impregnation + early Degeneration — crossing into the cell (nutritive layer, then organelles — mitochondria often the first casualty).
