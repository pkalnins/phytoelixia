Conceptual Models in Medicine

Every field of inquiry works from a conceptual model — an organizing picture of what the field's basic objects are, which questions are worth asking about them, and what counts as a meaningful finding. Thomas Kuhn's classic account of scientific paradigms captured exactly this: a paradigm is not simply a theory but an entire way of seeing, one that determines in advance what will register as a relevant fact, what gets dismissed as noise, and what counts as legitimate work within the field at all19. A conceptual model doesn't just describe a domain after the fact — it actively shapes what practitioners are trained to notice, which problems get studied and which get funded, how students are taught to reason, and, in a field with the institutional weight of modern medicine, how diagnostic and billing codes get structured and what insurers will and won't pay for. None of this is a conspiracy or a failure of rigor. It's simply what a conceptual model is: an unavoidable lens shaping the whole field's practice, not an optional add-on to some otherwise lens-free observation.

Medicine runs on two such models, not one. Pathogenesis is organized around the question of what causes disease and how to eliminate it; salutogenesis is organized around the question of what generates and sustains health, so that it can be strengthened directly. Most people, including many clinicians, use the word "medicine" to mean only the first of these, because one model built something the other never has. Biomedicine — the pathogenesis-grounded system of diagnosis and treatment built on modern anatomy, physiology, biochemistry, and pharmacology, and refined through the controlled experimental method — is a single, comprehensive, internally coherent structure. It can be taught the same way in any accredited medical school, practiced to a shared standard by any licensed physician, and advanced cumulatively through one common research literature that every new finding gets checked against.

Salutogenic medicine has no real equivalent. The traditional systems this workbook draws on — classical Chinese medicine most of all, but Ayurveda and the old European humoral tradition belong to the same family — are each individually rich, clinically sophisticated traditions in their own right. But each is self-contained: developed centuries apart, in different languages, on different premises, with no shared modern vocabulary connecting them to each other or to contemporary physiology. There is no single comprehensive foundation, comparable to biomedicine's, that a salutogenic practitioner could be trained in the way a physician is trained in biomedicine. This workbook exists to start building exactly that foundation: a comprehensive, modern-language salutogenic medicine, meant as an extension of modern science and biomedicine rather than a rejection of it — drawing its working vocabulary from Chinese pattern differentiation, Western herbalism, and anthroposophic physiology, and organizing it with the same systematic rigor biomedicine has long applied to the pathogenic side of medicine alone. The chapters that follow build that structure in stages: from shallow vs. deep salutogenesis, through the physicalist/idealist philosophical break, the fourfold human being, the three principles, the four ethers and etheric body, the seven life processes, the twelve organ processes, pathology, Reckeweg's own bridge between the two worlds, and closing with the therapeutic order that puts all of it into clinical practice.

Pathogenesis: The Study of Disease

Pathogenesis is biomedicine's organizing question: how does a specific disease state arise, by what mechanism does it unfold, and how is it eliminated? It is a question about the pathogen, the lesion, the mutation, the deficiency — some identifiable, nameable cause, traced from origin through mechanism to intervention. Modern biomedicine's institutional shape follows directly from taking this question as primary: Rudolf Virchow's cellular pathology relocated disease to a specific, visible site in the tissue rather than a systemic humoral imbalance1; the germ theory of Pasteur and Koch gave that lesion a discrete external cause; and the whole apparatus of differential diagnosis, targeted pharmacology, and evidence-based medicine that followed is built to answer exactly one kind of question well — what is wrong, specifically, and what removes it. (Module 2, "Worldviews," traces this history and its philosophical commitments in full.)

The success of this approach is not in serious dispute and this workbook does not treat it as such. Antibiotics, vaccination, surgery, trauma care, and targeted molecular therapeutics have done more to reduce suffering and extend life than any preceding medical tradition, and they did so specifically because pathogenesis asks a narrow, mechanistic, falsifiable question rather than a diffuse one. A clinician who cannot name what is wrong cannot reliably fix it. Nothing that follows in this workbook is an argument against that discipline — it is an argument that pathogenesis is a necessary but incomplete question, because it is built to explain disease and structurally has very little to say about health.

Salutogenesis: Antonovsky's Question

The medical sociologist Aaron Antonovsky coined salutogenesis — literally, the origin of health — as the deliberate mirror image of pathogenesis: not what causes disease, but what causes health2. He arrived at the question almost by accident. In a 1970 study of how Israeli women of different ethnic backgrounds adjusted to menopause, one subgroup was made up of survivors of Nazi concentration camps. Antonovsky expected, reasonably, that this group would show uniformly poor health outcomes. Instead, he found that 29% of the survivors rated in good emotional health — a meaningful fraction, though well below the 51% of the comparison group who had not lived through the camps3. The gap mattered. But so did the fact that it wasn't zero. Something had let more than a quarter of women who had survived the deepest imaginable pathogenic assault remain, by any reasonable standard, well.

Antonovsky spent the following two decades trying to name that something. His answer, developed fully in Unraveling the Mystery of Health (1987), reframed illness and wellness not as a binary but as a continuum — the "health ease/dis-ease" continuum — that every person occupies somewhere on, at every moment, under constant pressure from what he called generalized resistance resources: money, knowledge, social support, cultural stability, ego identity, and more, each of which helps an organism absorb stressors without being structurally damaged by them2. The construct that did the most explanatory work, though, was what he named the sense of coherence (SOC): a global orientation toward one's world built from three components — comprehensibility (the world makes cognitive sense), manageability (one has the resources to meet its demands), and meaningfulness (its demands are worth engaging with, not just endured)4. A strong sense of coherence, on Antonovsky's model, is not a claim that a person won't be exposed to pathogens or stressors — the concentration camp survivors were exposed to about as much as a human being can survive. It is a claim about what determines whether exposure becomes damage.

From the Psychosocial to the Physiological

Antonovsky built and tested his model almost entirely in the psychosocial domain — coping, stress appraisal, mental and emotional health outcomes. This workbook's central wager is that the same underlying logic — that there exists an organism-level capacity for maintaining order, function, and integrity under perturbation, separable from and prior to the question of what perturbs it — is not a fact about psychology specifically. It is a fact about living systems, and it applies with equal force at the physiological level: to what allows a cell, a tissue, an organ, an organ network to keep functioning coherently under metabolic, immunological, and structural load, not only what allows a mind to keep functioning under psychosocial load.

This is, in fact, closer to how most of the world's traditional medical systems already worked, long before Antonovsky gave the underlying idea a name. Classical Chinese medicine's whole apparatus of Qi, Yang, Yin, and Blood — the very vocabulary this workbook builds on — was never organized primarily around naming external pathogens (though it has a vocabulary for those too); it was organized around reading and supporting the body's own regulatory capacity, using constitution, pattern, and context rather than a fixed disease entity as the unit of diagnosis. The Western vitalist and eclectic/naturopathic traditions named this capacity outright, as the vis medicatrix naturae — the healing power of nature — and built their therapeutics around supporting it rather than around suppressing a named pathological process. Ayurveda's prakriti/vikriti framework and its emphasis on restoring dosha balance runs on the same underlying logic. None of these traditions used the word "salutogenic" — the word didn't exist yet — but structurally, they were physiologic-salutogenic medicine, asking what maintains the organism's own order, centuries before biomedicine asked the pathogenic question with comparable rigor.

Homeostasis and Allostasis: A Modern Physiological Salutogenesis

Western physiology has its own lineage for this same idea, developed independently of both Antonovsky and the traditional systems above, and it is this lineage — not Antonovsky's own psychosocial framework directly — that gives this workbook's physiological salutogenesis its most direct modern scientific footing.

Claude Bernard first proposed, in 1865, that the stability of the milieu intérieur — the internal fluid environment bathing every cell — was the precondition for what he called "free and independent life": an organism is only as free as its ability to keep its interior constant despite a changing exterior5. Walter Cannon gave that stability a name — homeostasis — and an operational description: the coordinated physiological processes, spanning the nervous system, the endocrine glands, and the viscera, that hold the organism's steady states within survivable range6. Classical homeostasis is a fixed-setpoint model: a variable (temperature, pH, glucose) is defended around a stable target through negative feedback.

Later physiology extended this considerably. Allostasis — literally, "stability through change" — was proposed by Peter Sterling and Joseph Eyer to describe something homeostasis alone does not capture: the organism doesn't just defend fixed setpoints, it actively shifts them in anticipation of demand — raising blood pressure before waking, mobilizing cortisol before a stressor even arrives — because anticipating and adapting is more efficient than only reacting after the fact7. Bruce McEwen and Eliot Stellar then named the cost side of this same capacity: allostatic load, the cumulative physiological wear that results when allostatic systems are activated too often, too intensely, or for too long, or fail to shut off once the demand has passed89. Read together, homeostasis and allostasis are a rigorous, quantifiable, thoroughly biomedical description of exactly the capacity Antonovsky was pointing at with the sense of coherence — except measured in cortisol curves and autonomic tone rather than questionnaires, and describing tissue and organ systems rather than a person's global orientation to their world. It is the missing physiological half of his psychosocial model, arrived at from the opposite direction.

Extending the Model: Neuroendocrine Immunology, Systems Biology, and Network Physiology

None of this stayed confined to endocrinology. Robert Ader and Nicholas Cohen's 1975 discovery that immune suppression could be behaviorally conditioned — by pairing an immunosuppressive drug with a neutral taste, then eliciting the same immune effect from the taste alone — demonstrated directly that the nervous system, and by extension the psychological state of the organism, could causally drive immune function, not merely correlate with it10. That single finding launched psychoneuroimmunology, the field mapping the bidirectional signaling between nervous, endocrine, and immune systems11, and it closed the loop this workbook needs closed: Antonovsky's sense of coherence is not a metaphor sitting alongside the body's physiology, it has direct, mechanistic, measurable neuroendocrine-immune correlates. A "meaningful, manageable, comprehensible" appraisal of one's circumstances is not just a mental state; it is a pattern of autonomic tone, HPA-axis activity, and cytokine signaling with real downstream physiological consequences — which is exactly the kind of claim this workbook makes, organ by organ, when it maps Yang/Qi/Blood/Yin activity onto autonomic, inflammatory, and structural pathways.

Two further scientific developments give this same logic its full expression at the level of the whole organism rather than one axis at a time. General systems theory, formalized by Ludwig von Bertalanffy, argued that living organisms are open systems best understood through their organization and feedback dynamics as an integrated whole, not by reducing them to isolated parts studied in sequence12 — the same organism-as-integrated-whole commitment this workbook's twelve-organ framework depends on. And network physiology, developed most systematically by Plamen Ivanov and colleagues, gives that commitment a direct, contemporary experimental method: mapping how distinct organ systems — cardiac, respiratory, autonomic, and beyond — dynamically couple and decouple from one another to produce a given physiological state, with the specific topology of those interactions, not any single organ in isolation, determining function and, ultimately, health1314. This is the same organ-network logic this workbook's own "Twelve Organ Processes" module builds on, and the same reason each organ chapter traces its Yang/Qi/Blood/Yin activity outward into that organ's relationships with the others — an axis, in this workbook's sense, is a network-physiology edge with a name and a clinical history behind it.

Bioelectricity and Morphospace: Levin's Extension

Michael Levin's research program pushes the network picture down to the level of the individual cell's own information processing, and gives the salutogenic idea — an organism-level drive toward a correct, self-maintaining configuration — its most direct experimental demonstration yet. Levin's central finding is that cells coordinate anatomical outcomes not only through gene expression and diffusible chemical signals but through bioelectric signaling — patterns of transmembrane voltage, propagated cell-to-cell through gap junctions — functioning as a distinct, "software"-like information layer sitting on top of the "hardware" of the genome15. That layer encodes what Levin calls a target morphology: not just what a tissue currently is, but what shape the collective of cells is oriented toward becoming or returning to — a target that can be read out, and even rewritten, by changing bioelectric state directly, without touching a single gene.

The clearest demonstrations come from planarian flatworms, which normally regenerate a single head after being cut in two. Briefly blocking the gap junctions that let cells share bioelectric signal — with a chemical, not a genetic edit — can permanently reset a worm's target morphology so that it, and its cell lineage, goes on regenerating two heads indefinitely, long after the original treatment has washed out of the tissue17; the same style of manipulation can even induce a flatworm to regenerate the head shape characteristic of an entirely different species, again with a completely unaltered genome16. The anatomical outcome, in other words, is stored and reprogrammable as a bioelectric pattern — a kind of pattern memory the tissue holds about what shape it belongs in — sitting alongside, not reducible to, the genome. Levin frames the full range of possible anatomical configurations a collective of cells could settle into as a morphospace, with development, regeneration, and even cancer suppression all describable as the process of navigating that space toward, or away from, a stable, correct attractor15.

This gives this workbook's salutogenic framing about as direct a physical mechanism as currently exists in the literature: an organism does not merely sustain damage and then get rebuilt piece by piece from outside — it holds an active, distributed, rewritable representation of its own correct configuration, and moves back toward that configuration given the right conditions, which reads as close to a literal biophysical restatement of the vis medicatrix naturae. It also sharpens what "supporting the body's own healing capacity" can mean at the mechanistic level: not simply removing an obstacle and waiting, but actively shifting the system's regulatory conditions — bioelectric, and, this workbook argues, energetic/etheric — toward the state in which its own self-organizing capacity can complete the work itself.

Functional Medicine: One Attempt at Network Thinking in the Clinic

Network physiology and bioelectric morphospace are basic science — they describe how the organism actually works. Translating that picture into an actual clinical practice, at the bedside, is a separate and much harder problem, and biomedicine-adjacent practice has already made one serious, large-scale attempt at it: functional medicine, which biochemist Jeffrey Bland and his wife Susan founded through the Institute for Functional Medicine in 1991, built explicitly on a systems-biology premise rather than the single-disease, single-target model pathogenesis-oriented medicine defaults to18. Its organizing move is to treat the body's major regulatory systems — assimilation, defense and repair, energy production, biotransformation and elimination, transport, communication, and structural integrity — as an interconnected network rather than a set of independently run organ specialties, and to look for the upstream, root-level imbalance connecting a patient's symptoms across systems rather than treating each downstream symptom as its own isolated problem.

That ambition is directly continuous with everything else in this section: it is, in effect, an attempt to build pathogenesis-era clinical infrastructure — careful differential workup, laboratory-verifiable findings, individualized treatment — on top of a salutogenic, network-level premise about what health actually is. In the same spirit of honesty this workbook applied to pathogenesis's own real successes, it is also fair to note that functional medicine draws real and repeated criticism for outrunning its evidence base: many specific functional-medicine protocols and supplement interventions have not been validated in rigorous trials, and the field remains largely unregulated and inconsistently practiced from clinic to clinic. That critique of particular protocols is not a refutation of the underlying premise — that the body's organ systems form an interconnected network, and that root-cause, whole-system thinking is a legitimate complement to single-target diagnosis is exactly what network physiology demonstrates experimentally, independent of any one clinical movement's track record. This workbook can be read as another attempt at that same underlying project, using classical Chinese pattern differentiation, Western herbalism, and anthroposophic physiology as its own working vocabulary for the network functional medicine is also trying to describe.

Taken together, this is the arc this section's chapters build out in full: from Antonovsky's original psychosocial question, through the physiological tradition — old and new — that answers the same question about the body itself, through the cellular-level evidence that an organism actively navigates back toward its own correct configuration, to the clinical attempts already underway to put network-level thinking into practice at the bedside. The organ-network framework the rest of this workbook is built on belongs to that same lineage.
