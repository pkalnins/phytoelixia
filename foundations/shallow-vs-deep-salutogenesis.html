Shallow vs. Deep Salutogenesis

An opinion piece.

Medicine has always organized itself around one of two questions. The first is: what causes disease, and how do we eliminate the cause? The second is: what generates and sustains health, so that it can be strengthened directly? Call these the pathogenic and salutogenic orientations. Modern biomedicine — its teaching, its research funding, its clinical training, its entire institutional apparatus — is built almost exclusively around the first question. The great humoral and traditional systems of medicine — East Asian medicine, Ayurveda, European humoralism before germ theory displaced it — were built around the second.

Anthroposophically-extended medicine (AEM) occupies an interesting middle position. It does not discard the pathogenic apparatus of modern medicine — the diagnostics, the acute interventions, the pharmacology — as somehow false or obsolete. It treats that apparatus as necessary but incomplete, and builds a salutogenic layer on top of it rather than in place of it.

I want to be clear at the outset that both orientations are needed, and neither is dispensable. Pathogenic medicine is indispensable wherever a rapid, targeted intervention against a specific, identifiable cause is what saves the patient: trauma, acute infection, a structural failure that needs mechanical repair. No amount of attention to self-organizing vitality substitutes for setting a fracture or treating sepsis. But salutogenic medicine is indispensable everywhere pathogenic medicine reaches its limit: chronic disease, prevention, resilience, the slow accumulation of dysfunction that has no single identifiable cause to eliminate. These are not two ideologies competing for the same territory. They are two different tools suited to two different classes of clinical problem, and a mature medicine needs both.

What I want to argue in this piece is that not all salutogenic medicine is equally salutogenic. There is a shallow version and a deep version, and the difference between them is not a matter of degree — more holistic, less holistic — but a difference in kind, turning on a distinction most people, including most practitioners, never explicitly draw: the distinction between a science's ontology and its epistemology.

Two Layers of Science

Science is both a model of what is real (its ontology) and a method for coming to know reality (its epistemology). Most people conflate the two, and mostly for good reason: they identify "science" with its epistemology — the scientific method, hypothesis and experiment, the discipline of testing a claim against the world rather than resting on authority or personal opinion — and they are right to hold that method in high regard. It is, after all, one of the most reliable error-correcting procedures human beings have ever devised.

But many people, including — and this is the uncomfortable part — many practitioners within the integrative and complementary medicine community, also unconsciously adopt the ontological commitments that have historically traveled alongside that method: not because the method requires them, but because they were never taught to distinguish the method from the metaphysics riding on top of it. That ontology has a name: metaphysical naturalism.

Metaphysical naturalism arose out of the Scientific Revolution in Western Europe, roughly from Copernicus's 1543 De Revolutionibus1 through Newton's 1687 Principia2. Its philosophical center of gravity is René Descartes (1596–1650), whose split between res cogitans (thinking substance) and res extensa (extended substance)3 first isolated the observing mind from a purely mechanical external nature — a move I'll return to below as the origin of what I'll call spectator consciousness. Metaphysical naturalism itself is the later, more radical descendant of that split: the assertion that physical matter and blind physical forces (gravity, electromagnetism, the nuclear interactions) are the whole of what exists, full stop, including inside the mind that first drew the boundary.

Unpacked, metaphysical naturalism commits its adherents to several linked claims:

  • Physicalism. There are no "vital" or otherwise special forces governing life apart from ordinary physical and chemical ones. Molecular biology and biochemistry are, in principle, fully adequate to explain life; life simply emerges from physical-chemical processes, by some mechanism not yet understood in detail. Organisms are elaborate machines, ultimately obeying the same mineral-level laws as everything else.
  • No causally efficacious consciousness. Consciousness arises from neural activity, which in turn reduces to molecular biology and genetics — the brain is, on this view, a kind of biological computer. How, exactly, remains the notorious "hard problem of consciousness," but the working assumption is that a sufficiently detailed account of the hardware will eventually dissolve the mystery.
  • No free will, no teleology. Purpose and agency, in biology as in psychology, are treated as folk illusions generated by, but not causally operative in, the underlying physical process.
  • Reductionism. The whole is nothing more than the sum of its parts. Living organisms — and consciousness along with them — are best understood by dissecting activity down to the molecular level and reassembling the explanation from there.
  • Mechanism. The activities of life and mind run on physical-chemical mechanisms; the human being just is a very complex machine.

There is a further question metaphysical naturalism never quite settles cleanly: are the mathematical laws we discover in nature literal features of a mind-independent reality, or are they human constructs — useful, predictively powerful maps that we should not mistake for the territory? Working scientists disagree with each other on this point more than the popular image of a unified "scientific worldview" would suggest, and I don't think naturalism as such forces an answer either way. I raise it mainly to flag that the ontology is less monolithic, and less obviously "just what science says," than it's usually presented as being.

That last observation matters more than it looks. Metaphysical naturalism is not itself a testable hypothesis. The scientific method can test claims about physical mechanisms; it cannot test the prior claim that physical mechanisms are the whole of reality, because that claim isn't a physical, empirically adjudicable claim in the first place — it's a metaphysical stance about what a physical finding is allowed to mean. It was simply adopted, as a working assumption, during the Scientific Revolution, and it has been carried forward ever since as though it were itself an empirical result rather than an interpretive lens. Most people who use it don't realize this distinction exists. I'd go further and argue that metaphysical naturalism arises out of a particular, historically conditioned mode of human consciousness — what I'll call, following the Cartesian split above, spectator consciousness: the stance of a mind that has withdrawn behind a pane of glass to observe a world it no longer considers itself part of.

Shallow Salutogenesis

This brings me to what I mean by shallow salutogenesis. I'd place a great deal of contemporary "whole-person" medicine here: functional medicine, systems-biology-informed integrative care, and — this is the part that will be less comfortable for some readers — a great deal of what gets called "energy medicine."

What makes a salutogenic approach shallow, on my definition, is not a lack of sophistication or a lack of genuine clinical value. It's that the approach remains grounded in the ontology and epistemology of modern biomedicine — metaphysical naturalism, and the measurement of physical quantities as the sole valid form of empirical knowledge — even while it reaches for a more integrative clinical picture.

To be fair to this work: whole-person medicine, even though it divides the human being into different levels of activity, is a genuine advance over strict reductionism, and functional medicine in particular can yield real insight into chronic disease that pathogenic models miss entirely. Systems biology and network physiology — treating the organism as an interacting network rather than a bag of independent parts — are the logical next step in medicine's own internal evolution, and I don't think that evolution is a bad thing. These are all legitimately salutogenic models. They are just still operating inside metaphysical naturalism's ontology, which sets a ceiling on how far they can go.

The ceiling becomes visible at exactly the point where practitioners start reaching for words like energy, soul, or spirit — because they reach for those words from inside metaphysical naturalism's frame, not outside it. And from inside that frame, there is only one category available for anything that isn't ordinary matter: energy, conceived on the model of physics — a scalar or vector quantity, in principle measurable, that does work. So life, consciousness, and spirit — phenomena that I'd argue are genuinely higher-order, that very plausibly obey their own laws and occupy their own "spaces" independent of, and not reducible to, physical space-time — get quietly flattened into that one available category. This is a category error, and it is the single most consistent tell of shallow salutogenesis.

Two examples make the error concrete.

Michael Levin's work on cellular bioelectricity is instructive precisely because it is rigorous, mainstream developmental biology, not fringe speculation. Levin has shown that cells navigate abstract, goal-directed "morphospaces" — anatomical shape-spaces — well before gene expression cascades occur; a bisected flatworm's bioelectric circuit recalls the target anatomical shape and can be made to regenerate two heads without a single base pair of DNA being altered.4 This is a real, empirically demonstrated non-local organizing activity. But notice: a morphospace is not itself an energy, even though it is expressed via measurable bioelectric voltage gradients. It's an abstract space of possible anatomical configurations that the organism's activity moves through, under something like a teleological pull toward a target state. Popular accounts routinely collapse this into "bioelectric energy," and in doing so, lose the very thing that makes Levin's work important — that it points to organizing information, not a subtler fuel.

The same error recurs one level up with psychological "space." A person's inner emotional and cognitive life — the structure of a Jungian complex, the geometry of what psychologists sometimes model as semantic or affective "space" — is not physical space, and treating "psychic energy" as a literal, if subtler, electromagnetic-style quantity makes the identical mistake Levin's popularizers make, just applied to mind instead of tissue.

Each of these domains — the etheric, if you like, and the astral — has its own proper laws, and lies genuinely outside the bounds of what "energy" means in physics. They are not attenuated, high-frequency, or otherwise "subtle" forms of energy. They are, in kind, beyond energy altogether. Shallow salutogenesis is what results when a practitioner senses this — correctly — but has no vocabulary available to name it except the one metaphysical naturalism supplies.

So: what's the alternative?

Deep Salutogenesis

This is, I think, exactly the place where Rudolf Steiner (1861–1925) has been most persistently misunderstood — not only by his critics, but by many of his own followers, and by practitioners of anthroposophically-extended medicine who import his vocabulary of "etheric" and "astral" forces back into a materialist frame rather than the idealist one he actually intended. Calling something "etheric energy" doesn't rescue the concept from the category error above; it just relocates the same error one level deeper into the tradition that was supposed to be the alternative to it.

Getting this right requires two separate expansions — one ontological, one epistemological — and deep salutogenesis, as I use the term, is what you get only when both are made together.

Expanding the Ontology: From Materialism to Idealism

The ontological move is to expand metaphysical naturalism into Idealism — and I mean a rigorous, contemporary formulation such as Bernardo Kastrup's (b. 1974) Analytic Idealism5, not a vague spiritual gesture. On this view, thought — mind, or consciousness itself — rather than matter, is the ground of the world. Matter is not "dead stuff" that somehow generates mind; matter is how mental, ideational activity appears when viewed from outside, through an external sensory apparatus. A living organism, on this reading, is something like a localized whirlpool in a single underlying stream of consciousness, not an assembly of parts that mind gets added to afterward.

Expanding the Epistemology: The Phenomenological Method

Ontology alone changes nothing clinically; it has to be paired with an epistemology capable of actually reaching the new territory it opens up. That epistemology is the phenomenological method originated by Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749–1832)6 and systematized by Steiner into an explicit ladder of cognitive modes.7 Where the ordinary scientific epistemology of measurement is built to grasp mechanism, phenomenology is built to grasp gesture, pattern, and — at its furthest reach — thought itself, directly, rather than only its physical residue.

Applied level by level, this looks like:

  • On the level of life, one moves beyond quantifying molecules and isolated pathways to perceiving polarities, patterns, and processes unfolding in time — an organ's or organism's characteristic gesture rather than its parts list. Steiner termed this relational mode of cognition Imagination, and it discloses real phenomena that purely intellectual, discursive knowing cannot reach at all, not merely reaches less efficiently: the etheric formative forces actually at work in a living process — the gestures of expansion and contraction, dissolving and hardening, that shape an organism through time.
  • On the level of consciousness, what Steiner called Inspiration allows direct perception of psychological processes, patterns, and archetypes — territory Carl Jung (1875–1961) arrived at independently, and partially, through his own concept of the collective unconscious and its archetypes.8
  • At a further reach still, Intuition allows one to grasp purpose, meaning, and one's own connection to activities and forces beyond the individual self — the point at which the separate gestures Imagination perceives and the patterns Inspiration perceives resolve into a single overarching archetype. Goethe's Urpflanze — the one archetypal plant-gesture underlying all of vegetation — belongs here, not at the Imaginative stage: it is not a further, more detailed gesture among others, but the unifying blueprint all those gestures were already expressing.

Cultivated through sustained practice and inner effort, these modes of knowing let a person perceive dimensions of reality that simply are not contained within ordinary physical space-time. Steiner, drawing on older esoteric and occult teachings, named three such dimensions: the etheric — the information-space holding and directing life-activity, which then projects into physical process; the astral — the information-space holding psychic and soul activity; and the devachanic — the information-space of pure thought or spirit itself.9

Beyond Energy: How the Subtle Shapes the Physical

Here is the crucial move that distinguishes deep salutogenesis from the shallow version, and that I think even many committed anthroposophic practitioners get wrong: these are spaces that interact with the physical world not through physical energies, but through something closer to phase alignment or resonance. The information held in the etheric, astral, and devachanic does not add energy to the physical system; it shapes physical matter and energy — including, for instance, the very bioelectric gradients Levin maps — as a formative template, without being identical to, or reducible to, that physical activity.

This is precisely the distinction that dissolves the category error. Practitioners in integrative medicine who speak of "energy" — the felt sensation of tingling or pressure while lightly scanning a patient's field, say — are attempting to frame a genuine, subtle perception using the only vocabulary metaphysical naturalism makes available. Physical energies are very likely involved as a downstream correlate. But they are being guided and formed by something more subtle and more fundamental than themselves; the pattern is primary, and the energetic trace is secondary, a shadow the pattern casts into physical space-time, not the thing itself.

The morphogenetic field is a good test case, because the term has real history rather than being purely esoteric coinage: it entered developmental biology in the early twentieth century (Alexander Gurwitsch's (1874–1954) "embryonic field" concept10), well before Rupert Sheldrake gave it a far more speculative reinterpretation as "morphic resonance" in the 1980s.11 What I want to insist on is the original, more modest and more defensible claim buried in the concept: this field is not itself an energy field, and not electromagnetic. It can shape electromagnetic fields in the physical body — bioelectric gradients among them — without being located in physical space at all. Steiner gestured toward this as counterspace, a notion his students George Adams (1894–1963) and Olive Whicher later worked out rigorously using projective geometry.12 It interacts with matter through non-energetic means I've explored at greater length elsewhere14 — phase alignment, and possibly the magnetic vector potential, are candidate physical correlates, though this remains genuinely speculative territory rather than settled science.

Astral fields — the basis of emotional and soul-level patterning — work the same way, and behave less like a fluid or a charge than like a phase field in physics or systems biology: they have topological structure, and something like attractor basins, in the sense C. H. Waddington (1905–1975) meant when he described development running down channels in an "epigenetic landscape."13 That image, notably, is itself a precursor to Levin's morphospaces — the same underlying intuition, arrived at from a different direction, decades earlier.

The Fourfold Human Being, and Why Modern Medicine Never Leaves Earth

Steiner laid out the fourfold human being on the basis of directly perceiving four distinct kinds of formative activity within physical matter, each governed by its own proper laws — activities we can label, for convenience, with the names of the classical elements: Earth, Water, Air, Fire. Modern biomedicine, and by direct extension every "shallow" salutogenic therapy built on its ontology, never actually leaves the Earth level: the level of fixed, measurable, mineral structure — even when its ostensible subject is life, feeling, or spirit.

Aristotle's Four Causes, Recovered

There is an older, and I think clarifying, vocabulary for the same diagnosis. Aristotle held that a complete answer to "why is this thing the way it is?" requires four distinct kinds of explanation, not one: the material cause (what a thing is made of), the formal cause (the pattern or organization that makes it the specific kind of thing it is), the efficient cause (the source of the change that brought it about), and the final cause (the end or purpose for the sake of which it exists).15

Modern biology and modern medicine, for all their sophistication, have narrowed this fourfold inheritance down to one member of the set. They answer "why" almost exclusively in terms of material cause — the molecules, the genes, the biochemical substrate a thing happens to be built from — and treat the other three either as eliminable in principle or as loose figures of speech for material processes not yet fully worked out. This is, in effect, the same reduction to "Earth" described above, restated in Aristotle's own terms.

The fourfold human being, read this way, restores exactly the causal structure modern science has narrowed away, one level at a time:

Aristotelian Cause Steiner's Body What It Explains
Material cause Physical The molecules, tissues, and mineral substance biomedicine already studies competently, and, under metaphysical naturalism, the only level it recognizes as real at all.
Efficient cause Etheric Aristotle's efficient cause is the primary source of a change — what actually brings it about. The etheric body is, quite literally, this: the ongoing formative activity, the "software" described earlier, that actively drives growth, regeneration, and repair moment to moment, without which physical process would have no direction at all.
Formal cause Astral Aristotle's own considered view, in De Anima, is that the soul just is the formal cause of a living body — not an extra ingredient added to matter from outside, but the very organization that makes a body alive and sentient rather than a mere assemblage of parts.16 Loosely mapped onto Steiner's more differentiated scheme, this is the office of the astral: what gives an organism its specific ensouled "kind," the particular form of sentient organization that makes it the kind of experiencing being it is.
Final cause Devachanic Purpose and meaning as such — "that for the sake of which," in Aristotle's own phrase — is exactly the register Intuition, described above, is built to grasp.

Modern medicine's blindness to three-quarters of Aristotle's own causal scheme is not a modern discovery superseding an ancient error. It is a modern narrowing, mistaken for progress.

Conclusion: Expanding Both, Not Either

Deep salutogenesis, as I'm using the term, is not simply salutogenic medicine with more spiritual language layered on top. It requires expanding medicine's ontology, from metaphysical naturalism to something like Idealism, so that life, soul, and spirit are recognized as genuinely real domains with their own laws rather than exotic byproducts of matter awaiting a future physical explanation. And it requires expanding medicine's epistemology in step, from measurement alone to the full phenomenological path — Imagination, Inspiration, Intuition — so that practitioners have organs of perception actually adequate to the reality the expanded ontology says is there.

Neither expansion alone is sufficient. An ontology without a matching epistemology is just an unverifiable metaphysical claim, no better founded than the materialism it replaces. An epistemology without a matching ontology is what produces shallow salutogenesis: real, disciplined, gesture-based perception, forced back into a vocabulary — "energy" — that was never built to hold it. Only when both move together do you get a medicine capable of treating the whole human being on each of that being's own proper terms, rather than translating three-quarters of the patient down into the vocabulary of the fourth.

Two Layers of Science

  • Medicine runs on two questions: what causes disease (pathogenic) and what generates/sustains health (salutogenic). Modern biomedicine is built almost entirely around the first; traditional systems (TCM, Ayurveda, pre-germ-theory European humoralism) were built around the second.
  • Anthroposophically-extended medicine keeps the pathogenic apparatus (diagnostics, acute intervention, pharmacology) and adds a salutogenic layer on top, rather than replacing it.
  • Both orientations are necessary — pathogenic medicine for acute/identifiable-cause problems, salutogenic medicine for chronic disease, prevention, and resilience.
  • Thesis: not all salutogenic medicine is equally salutogenic. The shallow/deep split is a difference in kind, turning on the distinction between a science's ontology (model of what's real) and its epistemology (method for knowing reality).
  • Most people conflate the two, identifying "science" with its epistemology (the scientific method) alone.
  • Many integrative-medicine practitioners unconsciously inherit the ontology that historically rode along with that method: metaphysical naturalism.
  • Metaphysical naturalism traces to the Scientific Revolution (Copernicus 1543 → Newton 1687), centered on Descartes's res cogitans/res extensa split — the origin of "spectator consciousness."
  • Its linked commitments: physicalism (no vital forces beyond physics/chemistry); no causally efficacious consciousness; no free will or teleology; reductionism (whole = sum of parts); mechanism (organism as machine).
  • Whether nature's mathematical laws are mind-independent or human constructs is a further question naturalism doesn't cleanly settle — a sign the ontology is less monolithic than usually presented.
  • Key point: metaphysical naturalism is not itself a testable hypothesis — it's a metaphysical stance about what physical findings are allowed to mean, adopted historically and then carried forward as if it were an empirical result.

Shallow Salutogenesis

  • Shallow salutogenesis = a great deal of contemporary "whole-person" medicine: functional medicine, systems-biology-informed integrative care, and much of "energy medicine."
  • What makes it shallow isn't lack of sophistication or clinical value — it's that it stays inside metaphysical naturalism's ontology/epistemology even while reaching for an integrative picture.
  • Real credit due: whole-person and functional medicine are genuine advances over strict reductionism; systems biology/network physiology are a logical next step. These are legitimately salutogenic — just ceilinged by naturalism's ontology.

Expanding the Epistemology: The Phenomenological Method

  • Ontology alone changes nothing clinically without a matching epistemology: the phenomenological method, originated by Goethe and systematized by Steiner into a ladder of cognitive modes. Where measurement grasps mechanism, phenomenology grasps gesture, pattern, and (at its furthest reach) thought itself directly.
  • Imagination (life level): perceiving polarities/patterns/processes-in-time — an organism's characteristic gesture — rather than a parts list; discloses the etheric formative forces themselves, not just a less-efficient route to the same facts.
  • Inspiration (consciousness level): direct perception of psychological patterns/archetypes — territory Jung independently reached via the collective unconscious.

  • These cultivated modes disclose dimensions outside ordinary physical space-time: the etheric (life-activity information-space), the astral (psychic/soul information-space), and the devachanic (pure thought/spirit information-space).

Conclusion: Expanding Both, Not Either

  • Deep salutogenesis ≠ salutogenic medicine plus spiritual language. It requires expanding ontology (naturalism → Idealism, so life/soul/spirit are real domains with their own laws) and expanding epistemology in step (measurement alone → the full phenomenological path: Imagination, Inspiration, Intuition).
